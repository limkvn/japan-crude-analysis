US Petroleum Market — Iran/Hormuz Crisis Monitor

EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report data • Updated through W/E 27 Mar 2026 • All data: U.S. Energy Information Administration

Context: On 28 Feb 2026, military action against Iran disrupted shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. Unlike Asian importers, the US has minimal direct exposure: Persian Gulf crude accounted for just ~7% of US crude imports and roughly 2% of total US petroleum consumption in 2024 — the lowest level in ~40 years. The US has been a net petroleum exporter since 2020. On 11 Mar 2026, the IEA announced a coordinated 400M bbl strategic reserve release, with the US contributing 172M bbl (the largest single-country contribution), deliveries starting 16 Mar over 120 days.

US Commercial Crude Inventories (excl. SPR)

Weekly ending stocks, thousand barrels. Crisis began W/E 28 Feb.

Unlike Japan's sharp inventory drawdown, US commercial crude stocks have built steadily since the crisis began — rising from 439.3M bbl (W/E 27 Feb) to 461.6M bbl (W/E 27 Mar), a gain of +22.4M bbl in four weeks. This reflects the US's minimal direct dependence on Hormuz transit crude and its position as a net petroleum exporter.

Source: EIA, Weekly U.S. Ending Stocks of Crude Oil (excl. SPR), series WCESTUS1

Strategic Petroleum Reserve

Weekly ending stocks, thousand barrels. 172M bbl release announced 11 Mar.

The SPR held steady at ~415M bbl from Feb through mid-March. The first small drawdown appeared in the W/E 27 Mar report (415,064 KBBL, down ~378K from prior week), consistent with the start of deliveries on 16 Mar. At the announced 172M bbl release over 120 days, the average draw rate would be ~1.43M bbl/day, implying SPR stocks could decline to ~243M bbl by mid-July if maintained at that pace.

Source: EIA, Weekly U.S. Ending Stocks of Crude Oil in SPR, series WCSSTUS1

Refinery Operable Capacity Utilization

Percent of operable capacity. Seasonal pattern: winter maintenance dips, spring ramp-up.

Refinery utilization dropped to a winter low of 88.6% (W/E 20 Feb) during the maintenance season, then recovered to 92.1% by W/E 27 Mar. This seasonal recovery pattern is typical and does not yet show crisis-driven disruption. Further ramp-up toward ~94-95% is normal for the spring gasoline season.

Source: EIA, Weekly U.S. Refinery Utilization, series WPULEUS3

Weekly Crude Imports — Total & Persian Gulf

Thousand barrels per day. Persian Gulf = Saudi Arabia + Iraq + Kuwait.

Total US crude imports have fluctuated between 6.2–7.2M b/d through the crisis with no clear disruption trend. Persian Gulf crude (Saudi Arabia + Iraq) has ranged from 604–916 KB/D, representing roughly 9–14% of total imports. These volumes are volatile week-to-week due to tanker scheduling and do not yet indicate sustained supply disruption.

Source: EIA, Weekly Preliminary Crude Imports by Country of Origin (release 4/1/2026)

SPR Drawdown Projection — 172M bbl Release Over 120 Days

Projection from W/E 27 Mar assuming announced release rate, starting from 415.1M bbl.

At the announced 172M bbl release over 120 days (~1.43M bbl/day or ~10M bbl/week), SPR stocks would decline from 415M bbl to roughly 243M bbl by mid-July 2026. This would bring the SPR to its lowest level since 1984, but still well above the IEA minimum 90-day import cover requirement (US imports ~6.5M b/d crude, so 90 days = ~585M bbl total stocks including commercial). Combined commercial + SPR stocks would remain above 700M bbl even post-release.

Source: Author projection based on EIA SPR data and IEA 11 Mar 2026 release announcement (172M bbl / 120 days)

US vs Japan — starkly different exposure: Japan imports ~95% of its crude from the Middle East, with the majority transiting Hormuz. Japan's commercial crude inventories have drawn down sharply since the crisis began, and its 45-day government SPR (at current draw rates) would be exhausted well before Japan could fully diversify its supply. The US, by contrast, is building inventories, has minimal Hormuz dependency, and is a net petroleum exporter. The primary US risk is indirect: global oil price increases feeding through to gasoline and diesel prices domestically.
