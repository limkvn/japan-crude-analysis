Japan Commercial Crude Oil Stocks: March 2026 in Historical Context

PAJ / METI monthly data (2002–2025) vs. PAJ Weekly (March 2026) — Iran Crisis Impact Assessment

Background: Japan’s Exposure to the Hormuz Crisis

Japan is arguably the major economy most exposed to the Strait of Hormuz closure. More than 95% of Japan’s crude oil imports originate in the Middle East, with Saudi Arabia and the UAE each supplying roughly 40% of total volumes. Japan, South Korea, China, and India together account for 69% of all crude oil and condensate flows through the Strait. Among these, Japan and South Korea are the most vulnerable: 87% of Japan’s total energy consumption depends on imported fossil fuels, compared with 20% for China and 35% for India.

The Strait itself is the world’s most critical oil chokepoint: approximately 15 million barrels per day of crude oil and condensate passed through it in 2024–2025, representing roughly 20% of global petroleum liquids consumption. When Iran effectively closed the Strait on February 28, 2026, it severed Japan’s primary supply artery with no readily available overland alternative.

This dashboard tracks the downstream impact of that closure on Japan’s commercial crude oil stocks using weekly data from the Petroleum Association of Japan (PAJ), benchmarked against 24 years of historical monthly data from METI.

Key finding: March 2026 crude inventory is the lowest March-end level in 24 years of PAJ/METI records. The Feb→Mar seasonal pattern normally shows a stock build (avg +471K KL) as refineries prepare for spring maintenance. Instead, March 2026 shows a 1.18M KL draw in just 3 weeks — a 1.65M KL deviation from seasonal norms, roughly a 3.5σ event. This is not seasonal fluctuation.

PAJ reporting suspension: On March 24, PAJ announced it would suspend publication of “Products Stocks (KL)” starting with the week ending March 21, citing that the Strait of Hormuz closure had caused “significant changes” in Japan’s petroleum product supply structure, making it “difficult to ensure the accuracy of certain published data under our current estimation methodology.” All product stock figures now read “n.a.” — retroactively applied to every week of March. When a national statistical agency can no longer estimate what is in the system, the supply chain has moved beyond normal stress into structural disruption. Crude stocks and refinery data continue to be published, but the downstream picture is now a black box.

Commercial Crude Oil Stocks

March-End Crude Oil Inventory (2002–2026)

Commercial crude stocks (excl. strategic reserves), kiloliters. 2026 value is PAJ weekly estimate for W/E 28 March. 1 KL ≈ 6.29 barrels.

The long-run downtrend in Japan’s commercial crude stocks reflects structural changes: refinery closures (capacity fell from ~5.0M b/d in 2002 to ~3.1M b/d today), declining domestic petroleum demand, and a deliberate shift toward leaner just-in-time inventory management. March-end stocks dropped from the 16–18M KL range in the early 2000s to roughly 10M KL by the early 2020s.

The 2026 bar is the lowest in the entire 24-year record, falling below even the COVID-disrupted March 2021 level (amber bar) by ~242K KL. Crucially, March 2025 had rebounded to 11.38M KL, so the drop to 9.33M KL represents an 18% year-over-year decline — not a continuation of a trend but a sharp break from it.

Seasonal Pattern & Weekly Trajectory

Feb → Mar Stock Change by Year

Positive = stock build (green), Negative = draw (red). 2026 uses early-Mar to late-Mar (3 weeks).

In 15 of 24 years, Japan builds crude stocks during February–March as refineries prepare for scheduled spring maintenance (turnarounds typically begin in April). The historical average Feb→Mar change is +471K KL.

The 2026 bar at -1,184K KL is the deepest March draw on record, 65% larger than the previous worst (2017’s -716K KL, which was a full-month figure). And this is only a 3-week measurement — the full-month draw will be even larger. Accounting for the seasonal norm, the deviation is ~1.65M KL, or roughly 3.5 standard deviations from the historical mean.

March 2026 Weekly Trajectory

Week-over-week crude stock drawdown accelerating post-crisis (Feb 28). Red line = stock level; orange bars = weekly draw size.

The most alarming feature of the March data is the acceleration. The weekly draw nearly triples from 235K KL in the first week to 631K KL in the third week. This is not a one-off adjustment; it is a system running down its buffer at an increasing rate as pipeline inventories deplete and no new Hormuz-routed cargoes arrive.

At the W/E 28 March draw rate (~631K KL/week, or ~4M bbl/week), remaining commercial crude stocks of 9.33M KL would be exhausted in roughly 15 weeks. The actual constraint will bite sooner, since refineries cannot operate on empty tanks — minimum operating inventories are typically 15–20 days of throughput.

Refinery Operations & Days of Supply

March Refinery Utilization (2015–2026)

% of designed capacity (3,110,400 BPCD). 2026 value from PAJ weekly W/E 28 Mar.

March 2026 utilization at 72.5% is the second-lowest in the sample after COVID-era March 2021 (71.1%, amber bar).

Interpretation note: Two hypotheses are consistent with the observed combination of low utilization and accelerating inventory drawdowns. (a) Involuntary constraint: refineries cannot secure enough feedstock and are forced to throttle back, yet still drain stocks faster than crude arrives. (b) Precautionary conservation: refineries are deliberately cutting runs to stretch remaining crude supplies, anticipating a prolonged shortage. Both interpretations are bearish. What the PAJ data alone can confirm is that utilization is depressed while stocks are falling fast — a combination that has no precedent in this dataset. Distinguishing between the two hypotheses would require data on unfilled crude purchase orders or refinery bid/offer spreads, which PAJ does not publish.

One additional observable: operable capacity itself dropped 70,000 BPSD in the latest week (from 2,982,400 to 2,912,400), suggesting some refinery units are being taken offline rather than simply idled. This is more consistent with an expected prolonged feedstock shortage than with a temporary demand-driven cut.

Stock Depletion Scenarios

Observed commercial crude stocks (solid) with three forward projections: March-average draw rate, last-week rate, and exponential-acceleration fit.

Rather than assuming a single constant draw rate, this chart shows three forward scenarios fitted to the four observed weeks of March 2026 data:

Scenario A (March average): Uses the mean of the three observed weekly draws (395K KL/week). Stocks reach minimum operating level (~3.4M KL) around mid-July (~15 weeks). This assumes the acceleration was transient and draws revert to the March mean.

Scenario B (last-week rate): Holds the W/E 28 Mar draw rate constant (631K KL/week). Stocks hit minimum operating level around late May (~9 weeks). This assumes the current pace continues but does not worsen.

Scenario C (exponential fit): Fits an exponential curve (draw ≈ 221K × e0.49t) to the three observed weekly draws, projecting continued acceleration. This is the most alarming trajectory — it implies stocks could breach minimum operating level within 4–5 weeks (late April). However, this scenario is physically bounded: draw rates cannot exceed available stock or refinery capacity, so it should be read as an upper bound on the pace of deterioration, not a literal forecast.

The shaded area between scenarios A and C represents the range of uncertainty. The true path will depend on whether alternative supply routes open, whether refinery runs are curtailed further, and the pace of SPR stock releases into the commercial system.

Strategic Petroleum Reserve Releases

Japan Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) Releases — Historical Comparison

Days of consumption released. Japan’s total reserves (government + private + joint) held 254 days of oil as of Dec 31, 2025 (470M bbl).

What is the SPR? Japan’s strategic petroleum reserve system, established after the 1973 oil crisis, operates on three tiers: government reserves (146 days of consumption as of Dec 2025, stored in national stockpiling bases managed by JOGMEC), private-sector reserves (101 days, held by refiners and importers under a legal obligation dating to 1972), and joint reserves with oil-producing countries (7 days). Together they totaled 254 days / 470 million barrels.

The 2026 release of 45 days’ worth (~80 million barrels) is nearly double the previous record: the 25-day release after the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake. It represents approximately 20% of the nation’s total reserves. PM Takaichi announced the release on March 11 — just 11 days after the Hormuz closure began — with initial drawdowns from the Kikuma National Stockpiling Base starting March 16.

For context, previous releases were responses to temporary disruptions: hurricanes, regional conflicts, the Ukraine invasion. The 2026 release responds to the physical closure of the strait through which >90% of Japan’s crude oil arrives. This is the first time the SPR has been tapped for a supply route severed at the source rather than a temporary supply interruption. The IEA simultaneously coordinated a record global release of 400 million barrels, to which Japan’s 80M bbl was the largest single-country contribution.

Even after this release, Japan would retain ~210 days of reserves — substantial in theory, but the rate at which commercial stocks are draining (~4M bbl/week) would exhaust the released 80M bbl in roughly 20 weeks if no alternative supply routes are established.
